Russia Halts All Gasoline Exports Until July 31

Moscow imposes a four-month gasoline export freeze amid Middle East conflict, drone strikes on refineries, and surging domestic demand — here's what it means for India and the world.

India imports crude oil — not refined petrol — so the direct hit to Indian consumers is expected to remain limited.

Russia has announced a complete ban on gasoline exports beginning April 1, 2026, which will remain in effect through July 31. The decision was announced by Deputy Prime Minister Alexander Novak, who instructed the Ministry of Energy to draft the formal legislation. The move is aimed at stabilising domestic fuel prices and securing sufficient supply for Russian consumers.

The trigger is a convergence of pressures: ongoing Ukrainian drone strikes on Russian oil refineries have disabled roughly 10% of Russia's refining capacity, while geopolitical turbulence from the Israel-Iran conflict has sent global oil prices sharply higher. Novak cited "significant price fluctuations" in global oil and petroleum markets as a key factor behind the decision.

Russia is the world's third-largest oil producer and normally exports between 120,000 and 170,000 barrels of petrol every day. Last year alone, it shipped approximately 5 million metric tons of gasoline to international buyers. The suspension of those flows will particularly sting nations that have come to rely on Russian supply.

Countries in the Eurasian Economic Union — Armenia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, and Kyrgyzstan — are exempt under intergovernmental agreements. Strategic partners like Mongolia and Uzbekistan are also largely shielded by long-term bilateral fuel deals. For everyone else, the scramble for alternative supply begins now.

This is not Russia's first export ban — similar restrictions were imposed in 2023 and again through multiple stretches of 2025, each time driven by a mix of domestic shortages and refinery disruptions. However, analysts note that the current ban coincides with one of the most volatile global energy environments in recent memory, adding greater weight to the decision.

Middle Eastern refiners, particularly in the Gulf, are expected to step in to fill the gap — routing additional cargoes to markets that previously relied on Russian supply. European refineries along the Mediterranean are also positioned to increase output. The net effect: higher prices and longer shipping routes for buyers.
